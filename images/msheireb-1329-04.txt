This photograph, taken from Al Najada Street in January 2014, captures the jarring juxtaposition between Doha's decaying historic center and its aggressive, multi-billion dollar modernization. In the foreground, a narrow, unpaved alleyway is flanked by dilapidated, single-story structures made of mud-rendered stone, cinderblocks, and corrugated metal. These crumbling buildings, once traditional Qatari homes or bustling shops, have fallen into deep disrepair, functioning at this time as overcrowded, substandard tenement housing for the city's low-wage migrant workforce. A lone man, dressed in a brown jacket, walks down the debris-strewn street past peeling plaster, exposed wiring, and basic storefronts, moving toward the starkly contrasting background.​

Looming ominously directly behind these impoverished slums is the massive scale of the Msheireb Downtown Doha construction project. Two towering yellow construction cranes, bearing the logos of contractors like Arabtec, dominate the skyline, actively assembling the sleek, white, monolithic structures of what would become the "world's first sustainable downtown regeneration project". The visual compression of the photograph makes the cranes and the new, pristine buildings appear as if they are physically bearing down on the crumbling alleyway.​​

This scene perfectly encapsulates "The Erasure of Msheireb." As the Qatari government moved forward with its visionary, $5.5 billion smart-city master plan, it systematically demolished these historic, run-down neighborhoods. Before the bulldozers arrived, however, thousands of migrant laborers were left to inhabit these severely degraded structures, effectively living inside active, encroaching demolition and construction zones. The photograph freezes this transitional moment: the exact point where the marginalized reality of the migrant workforce collides with the relentless, glittering ambition of Qatar's future.
