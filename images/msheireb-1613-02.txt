The facade of a dilapidated building on Al Jassasiya Street offers a bleak look into the housing conditions endured by many migrant workers in the historic Msheireb district before its mass demolition. The heavy, solid wooden door stands ajar, revealing an interior that resembles a gutted ruin rather than a home; the floor is covered in rubble, torn plastic sheeting, broken concrete, and discarded trash. Despite this level of structural decay and squalor, the exterior wall tells a story of active, ongoing habitation. A makeshift clothesline is strung across the cracking, stained plaster, heavily laden with freshly washed garments—casual plaid trousers, a blue towel, and bright yellow and blue work uniforms.​

This stark juxtaposition between the uninhabitable state of the building and the domestic necessity of the clothesline illustrates the desperate housing shortage for low-wage laborers in 2015 Doha. During this period, as the Qatari government moved to purchase and clear these old neighborhoods to construct the luxury "Msheireb Downtown Doha" smart city, workers were frequently left to inhabit severely degraded, overcrowded structures. Landlords often ceased all maintenance, knowing the buildings were slated for the bulldozer, yet continued to rent out spaces to desperate laborers who had few other options near the city center.

The photograph functions as a quiet testament to the resilience and marginalization of the migrant workforce. Forced to live in what essentially became active demolition zones, the workers still maintained the basic routines of daily life, washing and drying their clothes against the crumbling walls of a district that was actively erasing their presence to make way for a modern, glitzy future.
