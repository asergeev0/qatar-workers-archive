Plastered across a severely degraded, peeling wall in the historic Msheireb district, a makeshift message board offers a poignant look into the informal housing economy of Doha's migrant workforce in early 2014. The wall itself, stripped of its yellow paint and crumbling to the bare concrete, reflects the intentional, widespread neglect of the neighborhood as landlords ceased maintenance while awaiting the impending government bulldozers. Glued and taped to this ruinous surface are dozens of printed flyers advertising not just rooms, but individual "bed spaces". This specific terminology highlights the severe overcrowding that characterized these slums, where desperate laborers often rented a single mattress or bunk in a room shared by eight to ten other men just to afford shelter near the city center on their meager salaries.​

The advertisements also reveal a stark, self-imposed segregation within the broader marginalized community. The flyers explicitly stipulate "Keralites, only," "Filipino only," "Srilankan & Indian only," and even target specific local districts like "Kapilvastu & Rupandehi only" for Nepalese workers. In the highly isolating and precarious environment of the kafala system, laborers naturally clustered together by nationality, regional background, and language to forge essential support networks, creating tight-knit micro-communities within the decaying tenements.​

Framed within the narrative of "The Erasure of Msheireb," this fragile wall of paper underscores the extreme vulnerability of the workers. Driven into the city's most dilapidated spaces, they relied on these crumbling message boards to secure basic shelter and find community in a historic district that was actively preparing to erase their presence entirely.
