A group of four migrant laborers rests on the curb along Masafi Street in the historic, albeit decaying, Msheireb district of Doha in the early morning hours. It is 6:00 a.m. on a Friday—typically the sole day of rest for the workforce—yet the men exhibit a profound, almost paralyzing level of exhaustion. Rather than enjoying leisure time, they appear stranded on the pavement; two men sit with blank, weary stares, while another is slumped over his drawn-up knees, seemingly asleep or physically drained, and the fourth leans heavily against the adjacent wall.​​

The context of this scene in 2015 points to the severe housing crises that frequently plagued the migrant workforce during the redevelopment of Msheireb and other central districts. During this period, landlords and companies often suddenly evicted laborers from dilapidated, overcrowded tenements in central Doha to clear the way for massive modernization projects like the Msheireb Downtown development. Consequently, scores of laborers were frequently left homeless, forced to sleep on the streets or sidewalks with nowhere else to go until their employers secured new accommodations, often in the distant and highly segregated Industrial Area.

Their clothing—a mix of casual t-shirts, tank tops, and work trousers, rather than full protective gear or the "Friday best" often worn by workers on their day off—suggests displacement or a jarring disruption to their routine. The presence of a parked van nearby and the stark, utilitarian background of the street corner frame these men not as participants in the city's wealth, but as discarded collateral of its rapid urban transformation.
