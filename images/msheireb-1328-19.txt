This photograph, taken on Al Maymoun Street in January 2014, offers a stark glimpse into the severe housing crisis and profound neglect that characterized Doha’s historic center before its complete demolition. Slapped against a crumbling, unpainted plaster wall is a crude, handwritten wooden board advertising a "ROOM FOR RENT," covered in multiple, hastily scribbled and crossed-out phone numbers. The property it advertises is in a state of deep decay. A heavy metal gate and a rusted wire rack are draped with freshly washed jeans, brightly colored t-shirts, and work clothes, indicating that despite the ruinous conditions, the structure remains densely inhabited by migrant laborers.​​

In the background, the building itself exemplifies the intentional deterioration of the Msheireb district during this transitional period. The facade is stained and peeling, framed by a chaotic tangle of exposed electrical wiring, boarded-up panels, and a heavily rusted window air-conditioning unit. As part of "The Erasure of Msheireb," property owners—fully aware that their buildings were soon to be purchased and bulldozed for the government's luxury Msheireb Downtown smart-city project—frequently ceased all basic maintenance and repairs.​​

Yet, these slum landlords continued to subdivide and rent out these degraded, unsafe spaces to desperate, low-wage workers who had few other affordable options near the city center. This makeshift advertisement stands as a quiet testament to the exploitation of the expatriate workforce, who were forced to endure slum-like squalor and severe overcrowding within active demolition zones to build the modern metropolis rising around them.
